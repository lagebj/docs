# Capability Over Dependency

We prefer moves that leave customers more able to carry the work afterward. If a solution creates dependence on outside specialists or unowned tooling, it's suspect until proven otherwise.

## Key Implications

### Long-term Impact
Every solution should make the customer's future work easier. Our success is measured not just by immediate deliverables but by increased customer self-sufficiency.

### Tool Selection
Tools are appropriate only if they:
- Fit the actual customer context
- Reduce friction significantly
- Have clear ownership within the customer organization
- Don't duplicate or conflict with existing organizational structures

### Knowledge Distribution
Knowledge about systems and processes should spread throughout the customer team, not concentrate in a few individuals (including ours).

## Practical Applications

1. **Training Approach**: Every technical solution includes hands-on training for customer team members
2. **Documentation Standards**: All systems are documented comprehensively in customer-accessible formats
3. **Gradual Handoff**: Control transfers incrementally rather than at project conclusion
4. **Tool Rationalization**: New tools complement, not replace, existing customer competencies where possible
5. **Problem Solving**: Focus on teaching solutions rather than just providing them

## Measuring Success

- Customer team member confidence levels in system operations
- Reduction in support tickets from customer team to external resources
- Increased customer team contributions to system evolution
- Decreased reliance on external consultants for routine operations

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Creating "specialist-only" knowledge silos
- Introducing tools without adequate training and transition planning
- Becoming indispensable rather than enabling independence
- Focusing on impressive deliverables over sustainable practices
- Normalizing patterns that quietly transfer real ownership to external actors
